The 2012 Mets Need To Lose, In Order To Win

By Evan S. Schwartz in Featured, Mets, Top Story ⋅ March 9, 2012 at 12:39am ⋅ Post a comment

The Mets have a chance to accomplish something special this year: They may become the worst baseball team from a major city in the last 20 years.

Now, there have been bad teams from Chicago and Los Angeles, there have been disappointing squads out of Boston and the Bay Area, and even those cross town Yankees have faltered here and there. But the Mets are about to complete one of the most stunning falls-from-grace in recent baseball memory, a fall that is baffling by modern MLB standards. A team that, just a few years ago, had a top-five payroll, brand new stadium, owns its own network and is from the biggest metropolitan area in the country may be the least talented, least promising franchise in all the land.

It’s a special time in Queens.

It didn’t have to be like this. Remember, this team was a Beltran strikeout from the World Series, came within two gut-wrenching collapses of back-to-back NL East titles, and at one point had a left side of the infield that was the envy of the rest of the league. But everything that could possibly go wrong, both on the field and off, went wrong. Their star pitcher, who came over in an absolute robbery of a trade, broke down – and will cost $55 million more over the next two years. Their superstar shortstop put it all together, and will now put it together in Miami. Their billboard-ready third basemen went from the best in the league to middling, and may never recover. Their prospects got hurt, their veterans got old, their high-priced imports became Jason Bay.

Moreover, the Wilpons got wrapped up in the biggest money scandal in history. A team with unlimited money suddenly found their purse strings tighter than Kansas City. Now, having no money is not an excuse to lose in baseball, not anymore. Tampa Bay and Oakland and others have proved that money does not equal success – it’s competent general managing that breeds winning. But teams like Tampa and Kansas City and Oakland have all hit bottom time and again in an effort to build their farm teams, stockpile assets, trade talent for prospects and generally build homegrown teams.

The Mets have not drafted a real star since David Wright, and that was nearly a decade ago. They have dumped good money after bad. They have almost no tradable assets on the team, nothing in the minor leagues, and their best young player apparently has a fungus from living in the desert. They need to hit rock bottom, and fast. They need to follow a winning strategy, and they have to lose in order to do so. They need inspiration, and thankfully, there are some winning models in their own division.

The Phillies were in a similar position in 2000 – a marquee franchise, albeit one known for losing, bottomed out to last place in the division and won just 65 games. They drafted Chase Utley, who later became a cornerstone of their championship teams. The next year, they drafted Ryan Howard. In those years, they traded players like Curt Schilling and Scott Rolen, mostly for young assets, and drafted guys like Cole Hamels. They started to mix in veterans like Jim Thome, and by 2005 they were on the right side of .500 and moving towards the top of the division. Then Howard won an MVP, and they started spending big on pitching, and then they won a championship and reloaded just a few years later with Roy Halladay and Cliff Lee.  It took about seven years of slow building and smart drafting to get there, but the Phillies’ championship window is still open.

That kind of plan may be hard for the Mets to duplicate, but it would certainly be easier than the Nationals run to relevance. A team with unlimited money somehow stayed a laughing stock for the first six years in Washington, refusing to spend on big names and generally striking out on prospects. Then came a bunch of hits: Tyler Clippard in a smart trade, Jordan Zimmerman with a smart draft pick, Ryan Zimmerman with another great pick (and an affordable extension). Then came back-to-back transcendent prospects, Stephen Strasburg and Bryce Harper.

Yes, the Nats lucked out, but you have to suck for a long time to get two Number One picks in a row, and you have to be lucky that both of them were mega talented and major league ready. That allowed the Nats to turn “the future” into now: they spent big on Edwin Jackson, traded prospects for Gio Gonzalez, and may have a pitching staff to rival any in the league. This team lost 100+ games in back to back seasons, then 90+, sniffed .500 last season and may approach 90 WINS this year. Now THAT’S how you turn around a franchise.

Of course, getting lucky twice in the draft like that is incredibly rare, and the Mets won’t have the kind of money to drop $10 million on a fourth starter like the Nats do. Wow, what a weird sentence. The Mets have less money than the Nationals now. They definitely have less money than the Marlins, who have a tried-and-true strategy of winning the World Series, bombing out the roster, acquiring cheap talent and then winning again. Now that they have a new stadium, they set about poaching the worst-fit talent available and getting a manager who is, um, energetic. Their team is a Frankenstein’s monster of a squad, stitched together from mercenary types and former beloved superstars. They also happened to draft well: Giancarlo Stanton, Gaby Sanchez, and Logan Morrison are all products of the draft, and comprise the heart of their lineup. Hanley Ramirez and Anibal Sanchez were acquired when the Marlins gave up star Josh Beckett, another draft product.

Do the Mets have the kind of talent on their roster to pick up building blocks in a trade? Well, if they paid most of Johan Santana’s contract and could acquire one or two future stars in a trade, they should do in a heartbeat. Santana deserves more than laboring for a last-place team in Queens – he wrecked his shoulder for the Mets, and gave them three years of the best pitching they’ve seen since Doc Gooden. David Wright has probably never had lower trade value, and he’s a fan favorite, but if he has 15 homers by the All-Star Break, he’s as good as gone. He’s affordable, and still young, and should hopefully bring in a bevy of guys that will help the Mets rebuild with an eye toward… oh, I dunno. 2016? 2018?

Look at those other teams in the division: The Phillies, Nats and Marlins needed about seven years to rebuild, with varying degrees of bottoming out. If the Mets really, REALLY crash this squad, hit big with the 12 pick in the draft this year (no easy task) and can secure a top-3 pick next year, seven years seems about right. They need to acquire 3-6 more solid prospects for Wright, Santana, Pelfrey, Bay, or whomever has value on that squad – if they can enter 2013 with some young talent in the pipeline, they will be in good shape for the future. One day they will have the money to overpay for talented draft picks, and the city to attract free agents.

But for now, they need to gut the squad and get the bad times rolling. A jumpstart on rebuilding, if you will. If they dump payroll, and in three years can start building it back up to the top of the league, they will certainly have a shot. At blowing a division lead in September 2018.

The 2012 Mets! Underdogs!
